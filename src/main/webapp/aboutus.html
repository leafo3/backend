Why tropospheric ozone is a problem, and what you can do for helping: LeafO3

In the last decades air contamination levels have risen, due to industrial and urban development. This leads to create ozone at the ground level.

Ozone (O3) is a molecule formed in the troposphere when sunlight causes complex photochemical reactions involving oxides of nitrogen (NOx), volatile organic hydrocarbons (VOC) and carbon monoxide (CO) that originate chiefly from gasoline engines and burning of other fossil fuels. NOx and VOCs can be transported long distances by regional weather patterns before they react to create ozone in the atmosphere, where it can persist for several weeks.

Ozone in the air we breathe can harm our health—typically on hot, sunny days when ozone can reach unhealthy levels. Even relatively low levels of ozone can cause health effects. Breathing ozone can trigger a variety of health problems including chest pain, coughing, throat irritation, and congestion. It can worsen bronchitis, emphysema, and asthma. tropospheric (ground level) ozone also can reduce lung function and inflame the linings of the lungs. Repeated exposure may permanently scar lung tissue.

Tropospheric ozone causes more damage to plants than all other air pollutants combined!

It has harmful effects on sensitive vegetation and ecosystems. When sufficient ozone enters the leaves of a plant, it can:

  • Interfere with the ability of sensitive plants to produce and store food.
  • Visibly damage the leaves of trees and other plants, harming the appearance of vegetation in urban areas, national parks, and recreation areas.

In addition to reduced tree growth and visible injury to leaves, continued ozone exposure over time can lead to increased susceptibility of sensitive plant species to disease, damage from insects, effects of other pollutants, competition, and harm from severe weather. These effects can also have adverse impacts on ecosystems, including loss of species diversity and changes to habitat quality and water and nutrient cycles.

Although the leaves serve as biomarkers, scientist don't have an accurate idea of the extent of damage that has tropospheric ozone has done around the world. Data collection of large numbers of affected plants help to have a notion of whre the negative impact is generating the most.

What can you do for helping?

LeafO3 is an app that analyzes injured leaves by ozone on different plants and works like this:

  • You take a picture of an injured leaf using the app.
  • The app process the picture using an algorithm of image RGB segmentation and analyses it using clusters.
  • The damage it's calculated in percentage and classified in one of 7 classes of injury given by NASA.
  • The results are displayed on a map taking the geographic reference of where the picture was taken.
  • This can be used along NASA remote sensing products (satellital) to accurately determinate and understand the damage of tropospheric ozone around the world./li>

And that's how using LeafO3 you can contribute to help solving a global problem
